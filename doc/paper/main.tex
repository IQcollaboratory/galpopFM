\begin{abstract}
    We present the Framework for Attenuation by Dust Empirically (\eda) for applying
    dust attenuation 
    \tksedit{
         to galaxies from theoretical models. 
        \eda~provides a flexible prescription for assigning realistic dust
        attenuation to simulated galaxies related to their physical properties
        ($M_*$, $\ssfr$) and using the \cite{noll2009} attenuation curve
        parameterization. 
    }
    \ch{
        Using the \eda, we forward model synthetic observations for a $M_r <
        -20$ complete galaxy sample for three state-of-the-art large-scale 
        cosmological hydrodynamical simulations: SIMBA, IllustrisTNG, and
        EAGLE.
        We then compare the optical and UV color-magnitude relations, $(\gr) -
        M_r$ and $(\fnuv)-M_r$, for the forward modeled simulations to SDSS
        observations using likelihood-free inference. 
    }
    \tksedit{
        As expected, for all three of the simulations dust attenuation is
        necessary to match the observations.
    }
    %With \eda, we infer the dust attenuation curves as a function of galaxy properties so that the synthetic galaxy surveys best match the observed sample.
    We find that we can accurately reproduce the color-magnitude relations of the
    observational sample for TNG and EAGLE. 
    For SIMBA, we struggle to reproduce observations due to an overprediction
    \tksedit{
        of low-mass galaxies with high star-formation rates.
        }
    \ch{
        Examining the attenuation curves predicted by \eda~for TNG and EAGLE,
        we find good agreement with observed attenuation--slope relations and
        attenuation curves of star-forming galaxies.
    }
    \tksedit{
        Additionally, \eda~provides unprecedented predictions for the
        attenuation curves of quiescent galaxies, which are challenging to
        measure observationally. 
    }
    \ch{
        To match observations, simulated quiescent galaxies require significant
        UV and optical attenuation but with much shallower slopes than star-forming galaxies. 
    }
    \ch{ 
        Overall, we find that more massive galaxies in the simulations require
        higher dust attenuation, 
    }
    while galaxies with higher specific star formation rates have steeper 
    attenuation curves.
    In addition to improving our understanding of dust in galaxies, our results
    underscore the advantages of \eda~and the forward modeling approach.
